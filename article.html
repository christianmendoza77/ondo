Smart Contracts: The Engine Behind Every DeFi Protocol

Last Updated Date: May 21, 2026
6 min read
The Basics
Key Takeaways
  • Smart contracts are self-executing programs that run on a blockchain when predetermined conditions are met — no intermediary required.
  • They power virtually every onchain financial product, from stablecoins and DEXes to tokenized securities and lending markets.
  • Their value comes from being deterministic, auditable, and composable — but those same properties amplify risk when code is buggy.

Smart contracts are arguably the most consequential innovation to emerge from blockchain technology — and yet, for most people new to crypto, they remain abstract. The phrase itself is a misnomer: smart contracts are neither particularly "smart" in any AI sense, nor are they "contracts" in the legal sense most people understand.

What they are, instead, is something far more interesting: self-executing code that lives on a public blockchain and runs exactly as written, every time, without anyone's permission. That single property — deterministic execution without an intermediary — is what makes the entire DeFi economy possible.

What Are Smart Contracts?

A smart contract is a program stored on a blockchain that runs when predetermined conditions are met. Unlike traditional software, which executes on a server you have to trust, a smart contract executes on every node in the network simultaneously and reaches consensus on the outcome.

The concept predates Bitcoin. Computer scientist Nick Szabo proposed it in 1994, using a vending machine as an analogy: insert the right coin, get the soda. The machine doesn't know you, doesn't trust you, and doesn't need to — the rules are encoded in its mechanism.

"Code is law" became a slogan in the early Ethereum community for a reason: when a contract is deployed, its rules are absolute, and no central authority can override them.

— Vitalik Buterin, Ethereum co-founder

How Do Smart Contracts Work?

At a technical level, a smart contract is a piece of bytecode living at a specific address on a blockchain. Users — or other contracts — interact with it by sending transactions that call its functions. The blockchain's virtual machine (the EVM on Ethereum, the SVM on Solana) executes the code and updates the state accordingly.

The Three Guarantees

Three properties make smart contracts useful:

  • Deterministic — given the same inputs, the contract always produces the same outputs. No randomness, no ambiguity.
  • Auditable — the bytecode is public. Anyone can read it, simulate it, and verify what it will do before interacting.
  • Composable — contracts can call other contracts. This is why DeFi is sometimes called "money legos": entire products are assembled from existing primitives.

The Future of Programmable Finance

Smart contracts are entering a new phase. Three shifts are particularly important:

  • Formal verification — mathematical proofs that a contract behaves as specified, especially for high-value financial primitives.
  • Account abstraction — turning user wallets themselves into smart contracts, enabling features like social recovery and gas sponsorship.
  • Cross-chain execution — contracts that natively span multiple blockchains, removing the bridging step.

For institutions building onchain, the question is no longer whether smart contracts are robust enough — it's how to compose them safely with the rest of their financial stack.
